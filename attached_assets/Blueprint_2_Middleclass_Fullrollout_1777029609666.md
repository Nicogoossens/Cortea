# Blueprint 2 — Middle Class: Full Rollout
*This document defines Phases 4 and 5 — built after Phase 1/2/3 are complete.*
*Phase 4 covers what residents of a country need to navigate their own society.*
*Phase 5 covers what visitors or newcomers need — built last, optional for phase 1 rollout.*

## Phase 4 — The Country Layer (Residents)

**Purpose:** The codes that are unique to one country and that residents encounter in daily life.
**Audience:** People who live in the country, not visitors.
**Scope:** Built once per country. Cannot be reused across countries except as a template.

### Module structure

Every country gets the same 4 modules.
The module names are universal. The content is fully local.

---

### Module B — Regional Registers
*Universal concept. Local content.*

**Research Question:** How do social codes shift between the major cities or regions of this country?

**Structure:**
```
Phase 4: Country Layer | Module B: Regional Registers | [City/Region] | Level [N]
Bracket: [Demographic] — Level [N]: [Level Name]
10 Questions
```

**What to cover per region (3–4 regions per country is sufficient for Phase 1 rollout):**
- The dominant social register of that region (direct, warm, formal, nonchalant)
- How to read expectations when entering that region as an outsider
- What signals trust and reliability in that regional context

**Belgium example regions:** Antwerp · Ghent · Brussels · Liège-Wallonia
**Other country examples:** France: Paris vs. Province · Germany: North vs. South · UK: London vs. regional · USA: Northeast vs. South vs. West Coast

---

### Module C — Institutional Protocol
*Universal concept. Local content.*

**Research Question:** How does one interact with the formal pillars of this society?

**Structure:**
```
Phase 4: Country Layer | Module C: Institutional Protocol | [Institution Type] | Level [N]
Bracket: [Demographic] — Level [N]: [Level Name]
10 Questions
```

**What to cover (choose 3 most relevant per country):**
- Government administration (municipal, national)
- Healthcare (doctor, hospital, specialist)
- Education (school communication, parent-teacher dynamics)
- Legal / notary / financial services

**Note:** The specific institution names change per country (Stadhuis vs. Mairie vs. Town Hall) but the social dynamic — citizen navigating authority — is universal. Write the questions around the dynamic, not the institution name.

---

### Module D — Life Milestones
*Universal concept. Local content.*

**Research Question:** How does one navigate the high-stakes social rituals of this society?

**Structure:**
```
Phase 4: Country Layer | Module D: Life Milestones | [Milestone] | Level [N]
Bracket: [Demographic] — Level [N]: [Level Name]
10 Questions
```

**What to cover (choose 3 most relevant per country):**
- Grief and condolence rituals
- Wedding and celebration protocols
- Birth and family milestone customs
- Retirement and career transition
- Club and association life (verenigingsleven, associations, clubs)

**Note:** The specific rituals differ completely per country. The koffietafel in Belgium has no equivalent in Japan. Build each milestone from scratch using local knowledge.

---

### Module A — The Linguistic Map
*Country-specific. Cannot be templated.*

**Research Question:** How does one navigate language or dialect boundaries within this country?

**Only relevant for:** Countries with multiple official languages or strong regional dialects where language choice carries social weight.

**Examples where Module A applies:**
- Belgium (French / Dutch / German)
- Switzerland (French / German / Italian)
- Canada (English / French)
- India (Hindi / English / regional languages)
- South Africa (11 official languages)
- Spain (Castilian / Catalan / Basque)

**Examples where Module A does NOT apply or is minimal:**
- Japan, Poland, South Korea (effectively monolingual)
- Germany, France (regional dialects exist but language choice is not socially loaded)

**Structure when applicable:**
```
Phase 4: Country Layer | Module A: Linguistic Map | [Language Border / Context] | Level [N]
Bracket: [Demographic] — Level [N]: [Level Name]
10 Questions
```

**What to cover:**
- How to identify the safe first language choice in an ambiguous setting
- What the social cost is of choosing the wrong language
- When switching to English is the diplomatic solution
- How passive bilingualism works in that country (each speaks their own language)

## Phase 5 — Contemporary Context (Visitors & Newcomers)

**Purpose:** What a visitor or newcomer needs to navigate this country that is not covered in the universal phases.
**Audience:** People visiting or newly arrived in the country.
**Priority:** Built last. Not required for the Phase 1 rollout of any country.

### Module E — Digital Etiquette
*Semi-universal. Local thresholds vary significantly.*

**Research Question:** What are the digital communication boundaries in this country?

**Structure:**
```
Phase 5: Contemporary | Module E: Digital Etiquette | [Platform/Context] | Level [N]
Bracket: [Demographic] — Level [N]: [Level Name]
10 Questions
```

**What to cover:**
- WhatsApp vs. email threshold for professional communication
- LinkedIn tone (Belgian modesty vs. American self-promotion vs. German formality)
- After-hours communication norms and the right to disconnect
- Response time expectations per platform and context

---

### Module F — The Multicultural Mosaic
*Universal concept. Fully local content.*

**Research Question:** How do the country's majority codes interact with diverse cultural backgrounds?

**Structure:**
```
Phase 5: Contemporary | Module F: Multicultural Mosaic | [Context] | Level [N]
Bracket: [Demographic] — Level [N]: [Level Name]
10 Questions
```

**What to cover:**
- Entry rituals for non-native residents in professional settings
- Cross-generational code-switching (second or third generation navigating dual identity)
- Practical intercultural protocols (greetings, dietary considerations, religious observance in the workplace)

---

### Module G — Class Transitions
*Universal concept. Local social mobility dynamics vary.*

**Research Question:** How does one navigate a shift in social or economic class?

**Structure:**
```
Phase 5: Contemporary | Module G: Class Transitions | [Direction / Context] | Level [N]
Bracket: [Demographic] — Level [N]: [Level Name]
10 Questions
```

**What to cover:**
- Upward transition: how to adopt middle-class codes without losing authenticity
- Downward transition: maintaining dignity during economic difficulty
- The "imposter" dynamic: navigating spaces where you feel out of class
- Belgium-specific: the working class to middle class shift in Flanders

## Rollout Order for 20-Country Phase 1

1. Complete Belgium fully (Phases 1–3, then 4, then 5) — this is the master template
2. For each new country: copy Phase 1–3 structure, replace all cultural references and historical context
3. Add Phase 4 modules B, C, D — skip Module A if country is monolingual
4. Add Module A only if country has a genuine linguistic divide
5. Phase 5 is built per country based on demand — not a launch requirement
