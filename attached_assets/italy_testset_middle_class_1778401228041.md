Q1: Greeting the host at a business lunch
Scenario: You arrive at a business lunch in Milan and greet the host for the first time.

A) ✅ Good

You offer a brief smile, a handshake if appropriate, and say, “Buongiorno, piacere di conoscerLa.” You keep the first contact formal and let the host decide when to relax the register. In Italy, the safest professional default is Lei: polite, controlled, and slightly warm. The first exchange is not the place to race toward first names.

B) 🟡 Slightly different

You say “Buongiorno” and keep the tone courteous, but do not add much more until the host opens the conversation. Acceptable, but a little under-shaped for a business lunch: it preserves formality, yet it misses the small warmth that makes the opening feel complete.

C) ❌ Would not do that

You greet with “Ciao” and use the host’s first name immediately. This reads as premature familiarity. In a first-contact business setting, the shift to tu or first name is granted, not assumed.

Historical Context: In modern Italian professional culture, Lei is the standard polite form. First names and tu typically come later and signal relaxed familiarity rather than the default opening.

Q2: Rescheduling a meeting with a supplier
Scenario: A supplier asks to confirm a meeting time, but you need to reschedule.

A) ✅ Good

You apologize briefly, explain that something has changed, and ask whether another time would be convenient: “I’m sorry, something has come up on my side. Could we move the meeting to another time?” You keep the wording practical and respectful. Everyday Italian business politeness tends to be efficient rather than ornate. The key is directness wrapped in the correct form of address.

B) 🟡 Slightly different

You propose a new slot immediately, with a short polite opener, and keep the message concise. Still workable: the structure is polite, but the exchange is a bit more transactional and less carefully framed than the strongest version.

C) ❌ Would not do that

You send a casual WhatsApp message like, “Can we push it? I’m busy,” using tu and no apology. This feels too blunt for a first-line business adjustment. The issue is not directness itself, but directness without the formal wrapper that Italian professional etiquette expects.

Historical Context: In everyday Italian business settings, directness is fine when it is paired with Lei, a brief apology, and a clear practical request.

Q1: The formal first introduction in a prestige setting
Scenario: You are introduced to a senior figure at a private dinner in Rome or a gallery opening in Milan. The person is warm and friendly, but the relationship is still new.

A) ✅ Good

You strictly use “Lei” and surname/title until invited otherwise. You stay composed, polite, and slightly restrained, without trying to accelerate intimacy. In higher-status Italian settings, formality still carries real social weight. Warmth does not cancel the register; it lives inside it.

B) 🟡 Slightly different

You keep “Lei” but allow your phrasing to become a little warmer and more polished as the conversation develops. This is still acceptable. The essential discipline is preserved; the difference is mainly tonal, not structural.

C) ❌ Would not do that

You switch to tu or the first name immediately, or ask whether you may. This is premature familiarity. In refined Italian settings, the move to first names is usually granted, not requested, and certainly not assumed at first contact.

Historical Context: In Italian elite circles, especially in business, art, and institutional contexts, Lei remains a strong signal of formation and restraint. The shift to first names is a real relational milestone.

Q2: The first-name invitation
Scenario: At a foundation dinner in Milan, a senior host says: “Please, call me Alessandro.”

A) ✅ Good

You respond with measured warmth: “Thank you, Alessandro. Please, call me [Your First Name] as well.” You accept the invitation and reciprocate immediately. The invitation is relational, not cosmetic. In elite Italian settings, reciprocity matters because it completes the transition cleanly and avoids asymmetry.

B) 🟡 Slightly different

You answer with a touch more ceremony: “That is very kind, Alessandro. Please, do call me [Your First Name].” Still correct. It is a little more formal in tone, but the essential move is the same: accept, reciprocate, and continue naturally.

C) ❌ Would not do that

You keep calling him “Dottore” to show extra respect, or thank him without giving your own first name in return. This leaves the relationship uneven. The host has opened the door to a new register, and failing to reciprocate freezes the moment instead of completing it.

Historical Context: In Italian elite interaction, first-name invitations are meaningful social thresholds. A clean reciprocal move keeps the relationship balanced and prevents awkward asymmetry.
